Study Case

Representational harms from classroom LLMs

"Bias in narratives changes who students imagine themselves to be."

Quick take

Not all harm comes from automated grading. Sometimes the harm is how AI talks about students. Language models love patterns, and patterns can mean stereotypes. Ask a model to write "a story about a struggling student" and watch how often it assigns struggle to certain identities. It is subtle, but repeated exposure shapes expectations. Students internalize what the machine says about people like them.

Design guardrails with intention

Treat identity laden outputs as high risk. Avoid unsupervised activities where the model labels character, grit, or cultural background. If you must use role plays, script guardrails that prohibit stereotyped pairings and force neutral prompts. Better yet, make critical AI literacy part of the lesson. Show students how models can erase certain groups or default them to side characters. Ask them to redesign the prompt, then compare outcomes. That turns bias into a teachable moment rather than a silent nudge.

Test before class time

Teachers need a quick bias check. Before running an activity, generate several examples with different identities. If tone or expectations shift when identity tokens should be irrelevant, bin the activity or rewrite it. For feedback use cases, keep the human voice in charge. LLMs can draft suggestions, but the teacher should curate final comments, especially when identity might influence interpretation. If an AI cannot consistently treat every learner with dignity, it should not be the classroom narrator.

  • Run a six prompt identity swap test before publishing any activity.
  • Pair every AI output with a human authored reflection.
  • Store risky prompts in a shared folder so teams can iterate together.
